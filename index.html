⚖️ Environmental Legislation & Permitting Framework

Understanding 'Take' Under Tasmanian Law

Under Tasmanian legislation, the term 'take' is used to identify activities which are prohibited without authorisation:

  • For species listed on the Threatened Species Protection Act 1995, 'take' includes killing, catching, damaging or collecting any listed plant, plant part or product – including seed, foliage, roots, bark, sap etc.
  • For any flora species on land reserved under the Nature Conservation Act 2002, 'take' includes destroying, damaging or interfering with a native plant, plant part or product, including dead plant products such as wood, leaf litter etc.

Source: NRE Tas Scientific Permits

📋 When is a Permit Required?

Threatened Species

Native plant species listed as endangered, vulnerable or rare under the Threatened Species Protection Act 1995. In this case, a permit is required regardless of the land tenure (public or private land).

Public Lands

Any native plant species from public lands managed by the State Government (National Parks, State Forest, Crown Land, reserves) requires a permit.

Private Sanctuaries

Any native plant species from Private Sanctuaries and Private Nature Reserves declared under the Nature Conservation Act 2002, if you are not the owner of that land.

🔍 How to Apply for a Permit

  1. Determine if you need a permit using the criteria above.
  2. Read the Permit Application Guidelines – includes information on how to apply, fill out forms, and what happens after submission.
  3. Complete the Permit Application Form (Word or PDF format) available from NRE Tas.
  4. Submit to the appropriate section:
    • For Non-Threatened Plants from Reserved Lands: Ecologist, Natural Values Science, Natural and Cultural Heritage Division, GPO Box 44 Hobart, TAS 7001
    • For Threatened Native Plants: Threatened Species, Natural and Cultural Heritage Division, GPO Box 44 Hobart, TAS 7001

Contact: Flora Permit Enquiries – FloraPermit.Enquiries@nre.tas.gov.au

⚠️ Important Considerations

  • Increment cores and wood samples on reserved land for dendrochronology research have additional restrictions – contact FloraPermit.Enquiries@nre.tas.gov.au
  • Aboriginal and Historic heritage must be considered before any ground disturbing works. Failure to demonstrate due diligence may result in substantial penalties. See Aboriginal Heritage Tasmania or Heritage Tasmania.
  • Commonwealth areas in Tasmania: For permits covering Commonwealth land, see the Commonwealth Government Department of Environment and Heritage website for Listed Species Permits.

🗺️ Work Plan Development & Review

1. Desktop Survey & Species Selection

Florabank Guidelines: Essential Planning Steps

  • Desktop survey: AVH, Atlas of Living Australia, regional vegetation guides, local council databases
  • Species list refinement: consider seed storage behaviour (orthodox/recalcitrant), collection difficulty, quantity needed
  • Phenology research: flowering and fruiting periods from herbarium records, previous collections (Australian Seed Bank Partnership)

2. Work Plan Components (Detailed)

Mandatory Work Plan Elements

Species required: botanical name, common name, target quantity, fruit type (A/B/C)
Location details: property name, reserve, GPS coordinates (GDA2020), access notes
Timing: preferred collection window, backup dates, flowering status check
Permits: permit numbers attached, landholder permission letter, Indigenous consultation
Personnel: collector names, supervisor contact, emergency contacts (3 people)
Equipment list: collection tools, cleaning gear, PPE, vehicles
Transport & logistics: cool storage, delivery arrangements to nursery/seed bank
Risk assessment: WHS and environmental hazards signed off
Review date: scheduled pre-collection review

3. Reconnaissance & Site Assessment

Conduct site visit during flowering to:

  • Confirm species presence and population size (count plants)
  • Assess plant health and seed crop potential (estimate % of plants with fruit)
  • Mark up to 20 plants with GPS and biodegradable flagging tape for later return
  • Identify access issues, hazards, sensitive areas (wetlands, nesting sites)
  • Check for nesting birds, threatened species habitat (consult threatened species lists)
  • Collect soil sample for pH/texture if needed for provenance matching

Provenance: The Science of Local Adaptation

Why Provenance Matters

Plants collected from similar environmental conditions (rainfall, temperature, soil type, altitude) will have adapted to those conditions. Using local provenance seed increases survival rates in restoration projects by up to 300% (Florabank data).

Seed Transfer Zones: Many states have defined zones – collect within the same zone as planting site. In Tasmania, consider bioregions (e.g., Furneaux, Ben Lomond, West Coast).

Climate matching: With climate change, some practitioners use 'climate-adjusted provenancing' – collecting from slightly warmer/drier areas to future-proof restoration.

Uncertain Species Protocol

Step-by-Step Voucher Collection

  1. Collect representative specimen: 30cm branch with leaves, flowers if possible, fruits (if available)
  2. Press immediately in newspaper with cardboard, change papers daily for 2 weeks
  3. Photograph: whole plant, bark, leaves (upper and lower), flower close-up, fruit, habitat
  4. Record: location (GPS GDA2020), date, habitat, plant height, flower colour, abundance, associated species
  5. Assign temporary ID (e.g., "Acacia sp. Freer Farm")
  6. Submit to state herbarium for identification (may take weeks, fee applicable – contact herbarium first)

Above 2m Collection Methods

Pole Pruner + Ground Sheet

Extendable pruner (3-8m). Cut branches over tarp. Best for most species up to 8m. Requires practice to avoid branch damage. Ensure tarp is securely pegged in wind.

Ladders & Ropes

Orchard ladder (tripod) or climbing ropes with harness (requires certified training). Use for tall trees up to 15m. Ensure 3 points of contact, ladder on firm ground, spotter present.

Hydraulic Lift/Elevated Work Platform

For large-scale commercial collection. Requires operator licence, traffic management if near roads. Expensive but efficient for high-volume collection.

Equipment Care & Maintenance

  • Secateurs/loppers: Sharpen with diamond file after each trip, clean with 70% methylated spirits (biosecurity), oil pivot points with light machine oil
  • Pole pruner: Check rope condition (fraying), blade sharpness, extension locks functional. Clean after each use.
  • Tarpaulins: Shake out debris, dry thoroughly before storing, repair tears with tape or stitching
  • Bags: Paper bags – single use only; hessian – wash in hot water and dry; plastic – wash with detergent, dry, check for holes
  • Scales: Calibrate annually with test weights, check battery before each trip, zero before weighing
  • GPS: Update maps, carry spare batteries (2 sets), download waypoints before trip
  • Seed sieves: Clean with brush, check for bent mesh, store flat

Collection Quantity Calculations

How Much to Collect?

Rule of thumb: collect 2-3 times the amount needed, accounting for:

  • Cleaning losses (chaff, empty seeds, debris) – can be 30-80% depending on species
  • Viability testing failures (germination tests may show 20-90% viability)
  • Storage/insurance buffer (10-20% extra for backup)

Example calculation: Need 1000 viable seeds for nursery. Estimated cleaning loss 40%, viability 60% → collect approx 2,800 seeds (1000 / 0.6 / 0.6).

Weight estimation: Count seeds in 1g sample × total grams collected. e.g., Eucalyptus 500 seeds/g → 50g = 25,000 seeds.

♻️ Sustainable Collection Practices

The 20% Rule: Scientific Basis

Research (Florabank, 2021) shows collecting no more than 20% of available seed from a population ensures:

  • Sufficient seed for natural regeneration and recruitment
  • Maintenance of genetic diversity within the population
  • Food source for native animals (birds, mammals, insects) preserved
  • Population resilience to environmental stress

Application: Visually estimate total seed crop (e.g., count fruits on representative branches, multiply by number of branches). Collect ≤20% from each plant. For small populations (<20 plants), collect from fewer plants (e.g., 5 plants) or lower percentage (10%).

Ethical Collection Guidelines

Avoid Post-Fire Areas

Plants regenerating after fire need all seeds for population recovery. Wait at least 2-3 years post-fire, and only collect if population is robust (>50 plants).

Protect Fauna

Check for nests, hollows, feeding activity before collecting. Delay collection if birds are nesting in target plants. Leave seed on ground for ground-foraging birds and mammals. Avoid collecting from plants with active insect herbivory (may indicate stressed plants).

Biosecurity Protocols

Clean footwear, tools, vehicle tyres before entering and after leaving site. Use 70% ethanol or 1% bleach solution (30min contact). Prevents spread of Phytophthora cinnamomi (root rot), myrtle rust (Austropuccinia psidii), and weed seeds.

Environmental Impact Assessment (Template)

Pre-Collection Environmental Checklist

Check for threatened flora populations (consult EPBC Protected Matters Search Tool, Tasmanian TSP listings)
Identify and mark sensitive habitats (wetlands, moss beds, rock outcrops, threatened vegetation communities)
Plan access routes to minimise trampling and soil disturbance
Check for weed infestations – plan to clean down after leaving weedy areas
Assess soil erosion risk – avoid steep slopes when wet, use designated tracks
Check weather forecast – avoid collection before rain (mould risk), postpone if high winds (safety)
Plan to leave site cleaner than found (remove rubbish, flagging tape, food scraps)
Record any environmental observations (wildlife sightings, weed presence, fire damage)

Drying Protocols

  • Air drying: Spread in single layer on newspaper/trays, room temperature (15-25°C), low humidity (<50%), good airflow. Turn daily for even drying.
  • Silica gel drying: For humid conditions, use sealed container with colour-indicating silica gel (ratio 1:1 seed:gel). Regenerate gel in oven when saturated.
  • Moisture content target: Orthodox seeds 5-8% for long-term storage (can be tested with moisture meter). Recalcitrant seeds cannot be dried below 30-50% – sow fresh.
  • Testing dryness: Seed should crack/snap cleanly when bent, not feel soft or cool to touch.
  • Duration: Drying typically 1-4 weeks depending on seed size and humidity.

Storage Systems

Short-term (0-2 years)

Airtight containers (glass jars with rubber seal, ziplock bags with air expelled), cool dark cupboard (15-20°C constant). Add silica gel packets (change monthly). Check monthly for mould, insects, condensation.

Long-term (2-20+ years)

Dried seeds (5-8% moisture) in sealed foil packets or glass jars, freezer (-18°C) or fridge (4°C). Use moisture-proof containers. Thaw to room temperature before opening (prevents condensation). Ideal for seed banking.

Recalcitrant Seeds

Cannot be stored long-term. Sow fresh, or store moist (not wet) with fungicide in fridge (4°C) for weeks-months (e.g., rainforest species like Nothofagus, Elaeocarpus). Check regularly for mould/germination.
